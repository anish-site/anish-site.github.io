# From On-Call to the Shop Floor

I started my career holding a pager, keeping systems reliable. I spent my last
year running a business — sales, stock, suppliers, and customers. The path from
one to the other taught me more about decisions than any framework did.

## On-call teaches prioritization the hard way

When something breaks at 2am, you can't fix everything. You triage: what's
actually on fire, what can wait, what's just noise. Running a business is the
same problem in slow motion — a hundred things want attention, and the job is
deciding what *not* to do today.

## The shop floor teaches you who you're really serving

Engineering keeps you close to the system. Retail puts you face to face with the
person paying for it. You learn fast that customers don't care about your
process — they care that the experience was good and they'll come back. Lifting
retention from roughly 45% to 70% wasn't a dashboard metric; it was a hundred
small, human decisions.

## What carried over

- **Reliability is trust.** Uptime in software, consistency in service — same
  promise to the customer.
- **Measure, then move.** Whether it's an incident report or a sales trend, the
  data tells you where to push.
- **Own the outcome.** On-call or on the floor, someone has to make the call.
  Learning to be that person is the work.

The pager became a P&L. The instincts came along for the ride.
