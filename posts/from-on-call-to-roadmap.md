# From On-Call to Roadmap

I started my career holding a pager. I now help decide what gets built. The path
from one to the other taught me more about product than any framework did.

## On-call teaches prioritization the hard way

When something breaks at 2am, you can't work on everything. You triage: what is
actually on fire, what can wait until morning, what is noise. Product is the same
problem in slow motion — a hundred things want attention, and the job is deciding
what *not* to do.

## Empathy comes from the incident channel

Reliability work puts you closest to the moment a product fails a real person.
You read the angry tickets. You see the support load. You learn that the average
user doesn't care how elegant the architecture is — they care that the thing they
came to do actually worked.

That perspective is hard to fake, and it's the most useful thing I carried into
product.

## The skill I'm still learning: saying no

- An incident forces a clear "no" — you fix the outage, everything else waits.
- A roadmap rarely gives you that clarity. Every idea has a sponsor.
- Learning to say *"not now, and here's why"* — kindly, with data — is the work.

Moving from on-call to roadmap wasn't a clean break. The instincts carried over.
The pager just became a backlog.
